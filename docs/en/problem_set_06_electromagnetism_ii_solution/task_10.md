## Problem 10 — Eddy currents

### What are they?

Eddy currents are circulating currents induced in **bulk conductors** by a changing magnetic flux. By Lenz's law, they oppose the change in flux, producing a force that resists relative motion between the conductor and the field.

### Applications in braking

- **Trains and trams** — electromagnetic rail brakes; no mechanical contact, so no wear.
- **Roller coasters** — strong magnets on the track decelerate carts via eddy currents in fins.
- **Exercise bikes and gym equipment** — adjustable resistance via electromagnets.
- **Industrial saws and power tools** — quick blade stops for safety.

### Advantages

- **No physical contact** → no wear, no maintenance.
- **Smooth and silent** braking.
- **Easily adjustable** by varying the magnetic field (in electromagnetic versions).
- **No need for friction surfaces** that can overheat or fail.

### Disadvantages

- **Ineffective at low speeds** — braking force depends on $v$, so cannot fully stop the object.
- **Energy is lost as heat** — cannot be recovered (unless combined with regenerative braking).
- **Heavy magnets** may be needed for strong braking.
- **Heating** of the braked conductor — can be a design issue.

### Best materials

Materials with **high electrical conductivity** and **non-magnetic** properties:

- **Copper** — best conductor.
- **Aluminum** — lighter, cheaper, good compromise (used in most practical applications).

Ferromagnetic materials (iron, steel) also support eddy currents but exhibit additional hysteresis losses.

### How to increase the braking force

The braking force scales as $F \propto B^2$ and depends on conductivity $\sigma$, geometry, and velocity:

1. **Increase $B$** — stronger magnets (neodymium) or electromagnets with higher current.
2. **Use a better conductor** — copper > aluminum > steel.
3. **Increase the area** of the conductor crossing the field.
4. **Bring the magnets closer** to the conductor (field strength decreases rapidly with distance).
5. **Increase the velocity** — though this is usually not a design parameter.
